For the Bless'd Sunday Times, I -

Andrea watches herself in the mirror. She’s in a matching lemon two-piece suit, a gem found at the thrift store for $4. The shoulders are slightly too-big, and some would describe the colour as more of a soured buttery yellow, but she stands a bit taller in the suit. Her jaw sets confidently, her eyes alert.

Today’s breakfast lies in a bed of crumbs,

on a porcelain plate decorated with painted chilis.

She pulls apart the apricot danish and shoves a piece into her mouth. The crumbs drift into the white sink, one sits on a smudge of fresh mint toothpaste. An old make up collection sits on the countertop; observe missing lids, smudges, empty canisters. A beat-up notepad covered in blue scrawl is propped against a bottle of mouthwash. She flips the page, wetting the paper. The moment holds gravity, Andrea watches her face closely. She prepares her speech.

She pauses, watching herself mime an awkward laugh. No laughter comes out. He’ll say something cheesy, ask me if I’ve given up on my journalistic career.

“You’re always telling me that we should be writing things that are important. That we are the backbone of society because we can tell the people what they want to hear or what they should be hearing. And sometimes – actually, they never want to hear what we have to say. We are members of the human race – “

Dead Poet’s Society.

“We are all trying to change the world.”

Cheesy.

“This isn’t a story about shitty half-baked politics, it’s about race politics!”

Too controversial.

“It’s about the roles and responsibilities of Asian-American women in – “

Unconvincing.

Andrea pauses, catching her flustered expression staring back.

“It’s going to be funny. An opinion piece that pokes fun at Neo-Nazis who use right-wing politics as a front for their racist, xenophobic agendas,”

The words feel purposeful, right. She continues,

“It’s about the Neo-Nazis who think that dating an Asian woman is ‘a white nationalist passage’. About the Asian women who don’t mind dating Neo-Nazis. About the Asian women who are Neo-Nazis. We’re going Freudian. We’re going into childhood trauma. But it’s a piece that will be light, a perfect accompaniment to Earl Grey and parfait on Sunday. I don’t even want to condemn them. I just want to put their own shit under their noses.”

And Pete will smile, nod his head and say, “And they can’t even sue us because it’s opinion.”

~

I am being pushed out of his office. Pete, in watery brown eyes, sunk shoulders glory, is preparing to usher me out. There is a voyeuristic quality to my truth-telling which scares him.

“Wouldn’t it be nice to read something that isn’t political gossip?”

I wave the idea under his nose, knowing the very thought smelt right, like fresh cinnamon doughnuts in a caking of powdery sugar. Pete hates hedonism.

“Please - let me write this piece.”

I make myself small, defenceless. I know what draws men like “Slugline” Pete, Los Angeles Times Editor, from their burrows. It’s their misguided attempt at feeling like their egos can change the world. The male and literary ego are two sides of the same coin after all.

“You’re always telling me that we should be writing things that are important - ”

However, Pete is deep in his solitude, a borderline-retiree editor who has been miraculously blessed with a period of politics so densely fucked-up, in an industry so poorly funded, he’s one Adderall away from hitting the hay. He says no, with the strength of a packet daily affirmation.

“On Plenty of Fish, there was a reported spike in searches for ‘Asian women’ in San Francisco after Richard Spencer’s conference on ‘Nationalism for the contemporary American’ earlier this month. The event didn’t have any media coverage. Take a stab at how much that increase was?”

He takes the bait, “30”.

“48. Visits to the Klan’s website had an increased traffic up to 25%. This isn’t a shot in the dark. This is numbers, statistics, Google we’re talking about. The Klan is remodelling because three K’s just don’t sound as good without a Southern drawl. Young white college-educated men are holding Nationalist conferences, banning us from attendance. Jonestown hicks aren’t running marketing anymore. These are smart men who make a claim to non-racism with the accessory of an Asian wife on their arm despite underlining their favourite passages in their dog-eared copies of Mein Kampf at night.”

He looks up at me, “So you want to condemn them and their, or these women?”. He puts his mug under the coffee machine, it ejects with a sad putty sound, “Or is it because I’m Jewish and you’re Asian so we should start rallying against the Neo-Nazis?”

I swear I saw almost the ghost of pride on his face. He tells me that the story will take years off my life, pin me as a wack-job conspiracist, or worse, paint me as the poster child for batshit, crazy, unreliable, Liberal politics. At that stage, he says, I am the wound and he will personally amputate my festering body from the direct line of the reputability of the paper. I try to look cool and composed.

“I don’t even want to condemn them. I just want to put their own shit under their noses.” He laughs. The air feels golden with Pete’s laugh. He cradles his mug with child-like observation, “Fuck Grand Wiz Joe, or John, or whatever the hell his name is.”

“Exactly, I will give you something prophetic. Better than prophetic even.”

Pete sits in the kitchen, drinking his coffee, savouring the feeling I’ve given him. Nothing needs to be said. I’ve been given the green. I smile fondly at him and hand over a packet of Splenda. I know he’s watching his diet. I have cotton on my tongue. No words come out. The crack of my leather boots on vinyl floorboards lightens the hallway. I step out, my keys jangle, and suddenly I’m in the desert again.

~

I hit a dirt mound, My tiny Volkswagen Beetle creaks with orthopaedic urgency.

The red dirt road continues, long and narrow covered in tiny lumps and burrows. It’s an uncomfortable drive but my mind distracts itself, lingering on the image of Pete’s smile in the kitchen. He hasn’t smiled privately in a while. The last public smile was over a quick morning tea, accompanied with the news that there would be no more administrative casualties, but even then, it was performative.

In Pete’s heyday, he wrote a piece for the Times inquiring into the Bush administrations’ extraneous military activity in Iraq and the implications for the legacy trade war over weaponry which was ignited following the Soviet Union’s occupation of Afghanistan in the 1980s. It was an exemplar piece of investigative journalism and when he lectured at Berkeley in my final year, I felt a sense of awestruck. He was a celebrity in my eyes, shedding light into a brutish international policing and “probing deep into the psyche of American military involvement in the terrain of the Middle East”.

I knew deep-down, Pete felt like journalism was now an apeshit spectacle. Words were published before a proof and there was no longer a place for an editor who appreciated the old-world of print and ink. He would have been gladly let go, if not to mark the end of an era where writing was storytelling. “Let the kids eat the cake, we’ll all be dead soon anyway,” he would say.

~

Enroute is a small bar near the Canyons. From afar, it’s nested amongst the precarious rocks but drawing into the dusty parking lot, it’s just another bar. You would never guess that the bar was the go-to spot for The Doors and Hendrix in the 60s. It was now overrun with tourists making their way through America and the occasional writer in trail of the Kerouac’s and Ginsberg’s (the ones who had made American dirt romantic). The official Los Angeles tourism bus stopped there at 6pm on Wednesdays and Fridays too.

I sit in the corner of the room drinking a seltzer, looking in. The crowd is smaller than usual, but they fill the space. My drink is nearly polished off. In one fell swoop, she’ll come and whisk the glass away, leaving a ring of water on the table. The kind of little detail which would drive my mother into a fit of rage. But she’s the cool girl, she’s young and hip, to her, wood is wood, not a relic of a fantasy, love or past. Maybe she’ll smile at me. She’ll ask, “Another?” and I’ll nod, yes. We might just understand each other. The final gulp of seltzer goes down smoothly. I make eyes out the window, muss my hair a little, pinch my cheeks. To look drunk is to look in love. To look in love is to go through the world unfeared.

I’ll feign a drunken look of contemplation.

“Are you Dasha?”

She’ll be confused, perhaps she’ll wonder if she messed up my order. Her eyes will assess me, ponder if I’m a threat. But no, I’m just a slightly nervous, slightly drunk, harmless girl. Her voice will go saccharine with patronising sweetness.

I watch myself in the stained glass panes. My reflection looks back, positioned under a glassy angel catching Mary’s tears.

Eyebrows up, bambi eyes!

“I think I’ve seen you on my feed.”

Dasha Yoon was in fact, a face which flitted onto Andrea’s screen one night. A photo of blonde-haired, blue-eyed Republican poster boy, Spencer Archibald, yelling upon his podium had become viral overnight. Clicking on user dashay07’s profile on a ‘Make America Great Again’ Instagram post of this image, opened up the rabbit hole. An hour later and Andrea was 4 years deep into Dasha’s life, invested. She devoured pictures upon pictures of her perfectly artisan lifestyle – interesting cocktails, secret leafy nooks, pretty post-hike bare-faced selfies. These images padded her forefront love-story with Spencer, or ‘Spence’ as she called him. They were high school sweethearts. Contrasting his larger than life self, he seemed sweet in her pictures, posing under maple coloured fall leaves and walking their year-old Labradoodle. They radiated a genuine warmth which was infuriatingly aspirational.

Andrea flips through her notebook. reading over the scraggly scrawl along the margins.

She had a shtick – she was beautiful. And with beauty came power.

The line repeats itself as Andrea makes her way to the bar. The floor is syrupy from a menage of spilt cocktails. At the bar counter, Dasha wipes the maple countertops with a square of pink felt cloth. She has high carved cheekbones, and deliciously dark features. Her eyes are lined with dark kohl. The two girls make eye contact.

Andrea smiles, Dasha mirrors.

“Could I get another beer? From the tap’s fine.”

Dasha nods, yes. The beer hits the glass frothy. She taps once, releasing some of the trapped bubbles. She fills the glass to the brim with minimal froth. It was somehow an unexpectedly kind gesture. She smiles, setting the drink in front of Andrea.

“That’ll be $6.”

Andrea fumbles in her purse for a $10 note. Her fingers moved quickly around a lipstick, cards, keys.

“Are you Dasha?”

Her fingers graze a crumpled note.

“Yeah, have we met before?”

Inquisitive. Andrea smooths the paper between her palms.

“Probably not. My name’s … Candace. I follow your Instagram. I didn’t know if it was you, you look so different – with the eyeliner.”

Dasha’s expression softens, girlish.

“Do you like it? I’m trying a new look.”

Andrea places the crinkled bill on the counter. It sits defenceless.

“Definitely. It looks good. I’m actually here – I’m a journalist for a small paper. Um, we’re doing a review on this place for the Sunday section. Fun fact - did you know that The Doors and Jimi Hendrix used to party here?” The words tumble out articulately uncouth.

“I did, it’s the biggest selling point for working here in my opinion. I love The Doors. I can answer whatever questions you have. It’s dead now anyways.”

They turn in unison to observe the empty space. Only lone ranger types remained. Dasha pushes the bill back to Andrea.

“The drink’s on me.”

The conversation began with the beauty of the bar’s high arching windows. Dasha explained that she was upset the place wasn’t preserved as a church because she wanted to walk down the pews one day when she got married. I told her that sounded like a beautiful moment. Our conversation canvassed an assortment of topics. Dasha spoke with prosaic authority on the difference between farmer’s market produce and Wholefoods. We agreed cold cuts were only satisfying on a hot summer’s day straight from the fridge. Eventually, the question of our aspirations and goals came up, and I confessed brazenly, that I wished I was paid more and at a higher position at the paper. Dasha morosely looked around the bar, and exclaimed she wished she owned the place so she could transform it into something more boutique. “But he doesn’t want another thing to have to take care of – “

Her voice wavered.

The conversation dies,

I’m writing but I sense her studying me. She asks coyly, “You’ll scratch the last sentence, right? I didn’t mean anything by it. It just kind of slipped out.”

I don’t answer. I think -

She clicks her tongue.

The change is so sudden that only her eyes follow in the milliseconds after.

“I don’t want that last sentence published.”

“Ok, you have my word it won’t appear in the Chronicles.”

My face is burning as I tell the lie, I look down at the quick notes I’ve scrawled over my notepad.

“What paper did you say you were with again?”

I look up. Her eyes have grown dark. I do not break eye contact.

“Los Angeles Chronicles – it’s a small print local publication.”

She nods, looking away.

“I would really appreciate it if you left your notes from today.”

She reaches for my notebook. I recoil, cramming it into my scrappy leather bag. Her hand hovers, stunned.

“It’s off the record. I promise.”

She laughs. The sound is harsh. She pushes her body against the grain of the counter. Her face looms over, hostile.

“God, you’re a predictable bunch. Do you exploit your loved ones for the sake of a story too? You know what really stuck out to me when I saw you. God, this girl is really unhappy and unfulfilled, and she will never – “

I grab my bag from the grubby stool, focusing on the neon exit sign. The only touch of Modernism in this shitty, claustrophobic -

“Let me help you with your job as well, Candace. That’s probably not even your real name is it? Pathetic!” Faster. My boots lift of the sugary floorboards with a shlick. Her voice trails away -

“I’m assuming that you’re also a second-rate journalist so here we go - Enroute is a small bar located near the Canyons with a cosy and historic sentiment. The food and drinks are cheap. It’s a tourist favourite. But these people will eventually fall out of love with the idea of stale apple pie and brandy joints. This place will end up a pit in the Canyons. There’s your story. If you draw on your personal experiences of never quite being good enough at your job, or maybe in your relationships, you may be able to flesh it out. Hopefully, you can buy yourself a nice dinner on one of your lonely nights - ”

- to a silence. The doors swing shut with gusto.

I am outside and the lukewarm desert winds are choking me, Deep breaths, I try not to gag on the dust.

~

There is a moment during the pits of night in the Californian desert when the dirt shimmers and is what could only be described as a ‘moonlight mirage’. The heat slowly dissipates from the seared dirt and in those scenes, people claim to see Mojave ghosts charging at them. Others, unrest spirits and souls wandering the empty land like omens announcing themselves. Tales of Marilyn Monroe dancing barefoot in the charred dirt to Sharon Tate making daisy chains with the devil himself, became the stuff of urban city legend.

The opal moon floats in the rear-view mirror and my body relaxes. Nocturnal chinchillas hop across the cool dunes, eyes bright and white. It’s dark now, ghost cacti and bumpy dirt roads.

~

I open the doors, the lights are off. Sometimes I expect to see the small lamp by the window illuminated as if to say, I’m here, I care, you’re home. But to have those details there needs to be somebody else. I wonder how many texts, kisses, calls, dates it takes to make someone fall in love with a person only half-present. My keys tinkle, windchimes, as they hit the bottom of an Italian glass bowl my mother gifted me last Christmas. If that light was on, a small puff of dust would fly upward, snowy particles illuminated. Perhaps that would be a shimmering moment for me, something to journal down. To indulge in, before -

I stare at myself in the mirror. Green light illuminates my face despite my non-ownership of green lights. The moth wings, bodies and souls have collected in the low-hanging light, scattering the warm glow cold. I dip my mouth under the faucet, taking a long drink. A plate of softened crumbs adorns the counter. Half a danish sits sullen on the bare porcelain. The sink continues to run, eventually brimming with water.

I take a deep breath. In my home I see moths in the night, I submerge my face under,

Count 5 Mississippi’s
